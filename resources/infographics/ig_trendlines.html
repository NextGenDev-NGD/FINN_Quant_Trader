What a Trendline Is
Definition

A trendline is a straight line drawn along a series of price highs (downtrend) or lows (uptrend) to visualize the directional momentum of price. It is a dynamic S&R level — one that moves forward in time.

Uptrend line: drawn under rising lows. Acts as dynamic support.
Downtrend line: drawn above falling highs. Acts as dynamic resistance.

Minimum Requirement

A valid trendline requires at least 2 touch points to draw, and at least 3 touch points to confirm. Two points define the line. The third point validates that the line is not arbitrary.

The more touches, the more traders are aware of and reacting to the trendline — which makes it self-reinforcing.

Why They Work

Trendlines work because they represent the rate of change that the market has accepted. In an uptrend, buyers are willing to pay increasingly higher prices at a consistent rate. When price deviates below that rate, buyers step in.

They fail when the market regime changes — when buyers are no longer willing to maintain that rate of ascent.

A channel adds a parallel line at an equal distance from the main trendline on the opposite side. Price oscillates between the two lines. Trading range = lower line to upper line.

Trendline Breaks and False Breaks
Confirmed Break

A trendline break is confirmed when:

1. Candle closes beyond the trendline (not just a wick through it)
2. Volume is above average on the break candle
3. Follow-through: the next 1–2 candles continue in the break direction

All three criteria should be met before acting on a break. One condition is not enough.

False Break

A false break occurs when price pierces the trendline briefly — often with a wick — then reverses back inside the trend. This is one of the most common retail traps.

Caused by: stop hunts below the obvious trendline level, low-volume probes in thin sessions, news-driven spikes that quickly reverse.

Protection: Require a closing candle beyond the line. A wick through does not count.

Break of Downtrend Line — Bullish

When price closes above a confirmed downtrend line with volume, the series of lower highs may be ending. Watch for:

• First higher high to form after the break
• Retest of the broken downtrend line as support (role reversal)
• Entry on the retest with a bullish reversal candle confirmation

Break of Uptrend Line — Bearish

When price closes below a confirmed uptrend line with volume, the series of higher lows may be ending. Watch for:

• First lower low to form after the break
• Retest of the broken uptrend line as resistance (role reversal)
• Entry on the retest with a bearish reversal candle confirmation

Trendline Angle and Validity
AngleInterpretationDurability
Very steep (>70°)Parabolic move — unsustainable momentum. Often precedes sharp reversal.Short-lived. Do not project far forward.
Steep (45°–70°)Strong trend. Buyers/sellers are very active.Moderate. Often breaks but leads to a shallower trend resumption.
Moderate (25°–45°)Ideal — sustainable trend with consistent participation.Most durable. This is the zone where trendlines remain valid longest.
Shallow (<25°)Weak trend. May be consolidation with slight slope.Questionable. Price may be transitioning to sideways.

The angle adjustment problem: If you draw a trendline and then redraw it to "fit" more touches by adjusting the angle, you are curve-fitting — not finding a real level. Draw the line with the first two valid swing points and do not adjust it. If price doesn't respect it, the line is not meaningful.

Trendlines are context, not signals. A trendline touch tells you where to look for an entry signal. The candlestick pattern and volume at that touch tell you whether to enter.

Confluence multiplies reliability. A trendline touch that coincides with a Fibonacci level, prior S&R zone, or key moving average is a high-conviction setup. A trendline touch in isolation is one data point.

Three confirmed touches before acting. Two points draw a line. Three points validate it. Trade the third touch and beyond — not the first two.
