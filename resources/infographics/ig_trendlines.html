What a Trendline Is
Definition
A trendline is a straight line drawn along a series of price highs (downtrend) or lows (uptrend) to visualize the directional momentum of price. It is a dynamic S&R level — one that moves forward in time.
Uptrend line: drawn under rising lows — dynamic support.
Downtrend line: drawn above falling highs — dynamic resistance.
Minimum Requirement
A valid trendline requires at least 2 touch points to draw, and at least 3 touch points to confirm. Two points define the line. The third point validates that the line is not arbitrary.
The more touches, the more traders are reacting to the trendline — making it self-reinforcing.
Why They Work
Trendlines represent the rate of change the market has accepted. In an uptrend, buyers are willing to pay increasingly higher prices at a consistent rate. When price deviates below that rate, buyers step in.
They fail when the market regime changes — when buyers are no longer willing to maintain that rate of ascent.

A channel adds a parallel line at equal distance on the opposite side. Price oscillates between the two lines. Trading range = lower line to upper line.
How to Draw Trendlines Correctly
StepUptrend LineDowntrend Line
1. Identify swing points Find two or more rising swing lows — each low is higher than the prior low Find two or more falling swing highs — each high is lower than the prior high
2. Draw from the correct points Draw along the lows. Use bodies for cleaner lines; wicks for precise lows. Be consistent. Draw along the highs. All bodies or all wicks — pick one and don't mix.
3. Extend forward Extend beyond the most recent price — this is the dynamic support level to watch Extend beyond the most recent price — this is the dynamic resistance level to watch
4. Confirm with a third touch Wait for a third touch that respects the line before acting on it Wait for a third touch that respects the line before acting on it
Trendline Breaks and False Breaks
Confirmed Break
A trendline break is confirmed when all three criteria are met:
1. Candle closes beyond the trendline (not just a wick through it)
2. Volume is above average on the break candle
3. Follow-through: the next 1–2 candles continue in the break direction
All three. One condition is not enough to act.
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The three-criteria rule exists because each one filters a different type of false signal. The close filters wick noise. The volume filters thin-market probes. The follow-through filters news-spike reversals. All three together are not a guarantee — they're a filter that removes the weakest traps. Document which criteria you used on every break trade and check your IC by criteria count.
False Break
A false break occurs when price pierces the trendline briefly — often with a wick — then reverses back inside the trend. One of the most common retail traps.
Caused by: stop hunts below the obvious trendline level, low-volume probes in thin sessions, news-driven spikes that quickly reverse.
Protection: Require a closing candle beyond the line. A wick through does not count.
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The false break is a trade signal in the other direction — if you're watching for it. When price reclaims the trendline after a false break, trapped traders who sold the break are now underwater and must cover. That covering is the fuel for the continuation. Don't just avoid the false break — trade the reversal that follows.
Break of Downtrend Line — Bullish
When price closes above a confirmed downtrend line with volume, the series of lower highs may be ending.
Watch for: first higher high to form after the break · retest of the broken downtrend line as support (role reversal) · entry on the retest with a bullish reversal candle + volume
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The break of a downtrend line is not an immediate entry — it is an alert that conditions may be changing. The trade is the retest: price returns to the former resistance line, holds, and forms a bullish candle. That retest is your risk-defined entry with a clear stop below the line. The break alone chases a move that's already happened.
Break of Uptrend Line — Bearish
When price closes below a confirmed uptrend line with volume, the series of higher lows may be ending.
Watch for: first lower low to form after the break · retest of the broken uptrend line as resistance (role reversal) · entry on the retest with a bearish reversal candle + volume
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
A break of an uptrend line means the market has, for the first time, failed to make a higher low. That is information. Don't predict — observe. The pattern needs confirmation: a retest of the broken line as resistance, a bearish candle, and volume. Three signals, one entry. That's the protocol.
Trendline Angle and Validity
AngleInterpretationDurability
>70° (Very Steep) Parabolic — unsustainable momentum. Often precedes a sharp reversal. Short-lived. Do not project far forward.
45–70° (Steep) Strong trend. Buyers/sellers very active. Moderate. Often breaks but leads to a shallower trend resumption.
25–45° (Moderate) Ideal — sustainable with consistent participation. Most durable. Trendlines remain valid longest in this zone.
<25° (Shallow) Weak trend. May be consolidation with slight slope. Questionable. Price may be transitioning to sideways.
⬡ FINN
"The angle adjustment problem: if you draw a trendline and then redraw it to 'fit' more touches by adjusting the angle, you are curve-fitting — not finding a real level. Draw the line with the first two valid swing points and do not adjust it. If price doesn't respect it, the line is not meaningful. The market is not obligated to fit your drawing."
— FINN: Trendlines are context, not signals. The candle tells you whether to act.
FINN Application Rules
Context, Not Signal
A trendline touch tells you where to look for an entry. The candlestick pattern and volume at that touch tell you whether to enter. Trendline alone = one data point.
Three Touches Before Trading
Two points draw a line. Three points validate it. Trade the third touch and beyond — not the first two. The third touch is the confirmation that other traders see it too.
Confluence Multiplies Reliability
A trendline touch at a Fibonacci level + prior S&R zone + key MA = high-conviction setup. Trendline in isolation = one data point. Stack the evidence before entering.
Don't Redraw to Fit
Draw with the first two valid swings and hold it fixed. Adjusting the angle to get more touches is curve-fitting. A line that needs adjustment to "work" isn't working — it's confirmation bias.
