Bearish

Structure Two peaks at approximately equal price. Trough between peaks forms the neckline. Second peak should not exceed the first by more than ~2%.

Bullish Reversals — Extended Formation Patterns

Cup and Handle

Bullish
──────────────── ← Rim / Neckline (prior highs) \ / \ / ← Cup (U-shape, ~3–6 months) \ / \________/ ← Cup base \ / v ← Handle (tight consolidation, | −5% to −12% pullback) | ^ ← Breakout above rim
Structure Prior uptrend → rounded U-shaped decline (cup) → tight high-level consolidation (handle). Handle should be in upper 50% of the cup and slope downward mildly. Cup depth: ideally 15–30%; handle: 5–12%.
Duration Cup: 3–6 months (ideal). Handle: 1–4 weeks. Works on shorter timeframes with proportionally shorter durations.

Stop Below handle low. Wider cup = wider stop needed — size position accordingly.
Confirm Volume surge on breakout Clean U-shape (not V-shape) Prior uptrend exists

Note Popularized by William O'Neil (CANSLIM). Best on weekly chart in strong sectors with high RS stocks.

Rounding Bottom

Bullish
\ / \ / \ / ← Slow, gradual turn \ / (months to years) \ / \ / ────── ← Extended base (saucer) / / ^ ← Acceleration as price breaks prior highs
Structure Very gradual, arc-shaped base. No sharp V-turns. The longer the base, the more powerful the eventual breakout. Common on weekly/monthly charts.
Duration Weeks to months. Shorter versions on daily charts are shallower saucers.

Entry Break above the left-side high (pattern lip). Can also enter on right-arc upturn if confirmed by increasing volume.

Target = Breakout + Pattern Height (lip − base)
Confirm Gradual arc (not V-shape) Volume dries during base Volume uptick on right arc
Invalidate Sharp V-recovery (not a rounding); new lower low during base phase

The Neckline — Why It Matters for Every Topping/Bottoming Pattern

The neckline is the support/resistance line formed by connecting the intermediate troughs (in Double Top, H&S) or peaks (in Double Bottom, IH&S). It is not just a visual marker — it is the trade trigger line. Enter only on a confirmed close beyond the neckline, not on an intraday touch. Neckline retests (price comes back to test the broken neckline from the other side) are the highest-probability entry points and should always be watched for after a breakout. If the retest fails (price breaks back through the neckline), exit the position — the pattern has failed.

Pattern Anatomy — Key Components
Peak / Trough
Pivot Point
The key high or low that defines the pattern. Must be a clear, discrete swing — not a gradual drift.
Neckline
Trade Trigger
The line connecting intermediate swings. Break of neckline = entry signal. Retest = confirmation entry.
Pattern Height
Target Input
Distance from neckline to the most extreme peak/trough. Projects the minimum expected move after breakout.
Right Side
Quality Check
Right shoulder (H&S) or second peak/trough should show declining volume vs. the left side — this signals exhaustion.
Breakout Bar
Volume Signal
The candle that breaks the neckline should have volume ≥ 50% above the 20-day average. Low-volume breaks fail often.
Handle
C&H Specific
Tight consolidation after the cup's right lip. Must stay in the upper half of the cup — low handles weaken the setup.

FINN Rules — Reversal Patterns
Rule 1
Wait for the close
Intraday neckline touches are noise. Only a daily (or relevant timeframe) close beyond the neckline counts as a trigger.
Rule 2
Volume must confirm
A neckline break on below-average volume has a high failure rate. If volume doesn't spike, don't trade it.
Rule 3
Target ≠ guarantee
The measured target is a probability zone, not a certainty. Take partial profits at 50–75% of target and trail the rest.
Rule 4
Symmetry matters
A visually clean, symmetric pattern (equal peaks, clean arc) has higher reliability than a forced or lopsided one.
Rule 5
Context over pattern
A bearish reversal in a strong bull market has lower odds than in a weakening sector. Pattern + context = signal. Pattern alone = noise.
Rule 6
Log invalidations
When a pattern fails (price reverses back through neckline), log it. Wrong predictions are data — they refine your read.
