The 3 Dangerous Emotions — How They Destroy Edge
Fear
"This could go against me."
Danger Level 0%
Core Mechanism
Brain registers unrealized loss as a threat. The stress response fires before any actual loss occurs.
How It Manifests
Exiting winning trades early. Failing to enter valid setups. Cutting positions during normal pullbacks in a good trade.
The Real Cost
Systematically truncated right tail — big wins never develop. Win rate looks fine; R-multiple distribution is broken.
FINN Counter
Set target and stop before entering. Write them in the journal. Once in trade, manage to the plan — not to the feeling.
Greed
"It's going to keep going."
Danger Level 0%
Core Mechanism
Winning activates dopamine. Brain seeks more stimulus. Risk tolerance inflates after wins, deflates after losses — opposite of rational.
How It Manifests
Holding winners past targets. Increasing size after a good week. Adding to losing positions. Chasing stocks already extended from setup.
The Real Cost
Winners become losers. Account equity develops left skew — occasional large losses wipe out multiple wins.
FINN Counter
Targets are pre-set rules, not feelings. The target was set when you were thinking clearly — honor the plan made before the trade.
Revenge Trading
"I need to make it back."
Danger Level 0%
Core Mechanism
After a loss, brain frames recovery as restoring justice rather than managing risk. Trading becomes emotional, not analytical.
How It Manifests
Entering immediately after a loss without a valid setup. Increasing size to "make it back faster." Abandoning all rules because they "don't work."
The Real Cost
Worst trading days are 3–4 bad trades in sequence, not one. First loss is small; revenge trading converts it into a drawdown that takes weeks to recover.
FINN Counter
The daily loss limit rule exists for this moment. When you hit the limit, you stop — no exceptions. Close the platform. Log the loss. Come back tomorrow.
Cognitive Biases — Click Each Card to Reveal (Bias → Manifestation → Countermeasure)
Confirmation Bias
Seeking information that confirms existing belief; actively dismissing contradictory data.
HIGH tap to flip →
In Trading
Bullish on a stock → ignoring bearish signals, only reading bullish news, avoiding charts that show weakness.
FINN Counter
Before each trade, list the top 2 reasons the thesis is wrong. What would the bears say? Run the IC.
"What does the data say against this?"
Recency Bias
Overweighting recent events relative to the long-run base rate.
HIGH tap to flip →
In Trading
After a winning week → overconfidence, sizing up without statistical basis. After a losing week → abandoning valid strategies.
FINN Counter
Evaluate strategies over the full backtest period, not last week. Require 30+ trade sample before assessing edge.
"Show me the IC over 30+ trades."
Loss Aversion
Losses feel ~2× as painful as equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman-Tversky, 1979).
HIGH tap to flip →
In Trading
Holding losers too long to avoid "realizing" the loss. Taking profits early. Asymmetric decisions that systematically harm R/R.
FINN Counter
Pre-set stops and targets. Once in a trade, P&L display is noise — only price relative to your levels matters.
"The stop was set when I was thinking clearly."
Sunk Cost Fallacy
Continuing an action because of past investment rather than future merit.
MEDIUM tap to flip →
In Trading
"I already lost $800 on this — I can't sell now." Position held because of what was already lost, not what the data says forward.
FINN Counter
The market doesn't know your entry price. Ask: Would I enter this position fresh at today's price? If no — exit.
"My entry price is irrelevant to forward probability."
Anchoring
Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered as a reference point.
MEDIUM tap to flip →
In Trading
Stock bought at $50 → still anchored to $50 as "value" even after fundamental deterioration. Entry price no longer has structural meaning.
FINN Counter
Evaluate the stock where it is now, not where you bought it. Use the same entry criteria you'd apply to a brand-new position.
"What would I do with this if I were new to it?"
Overconfidence
Overestimating predictive ability and underestimating uncertainty.
MEDIUM tap to flip →
In Trading
Sizing up after wins. Skipping confirmation steps. "I know this one is going to work." The IC was 0.04 — the confidence is 0.85.
FINN Counter
Data provides the confidence level, not your feeling. Run the IC. Check the OOS result. Confidence is earned by numbers, not felt.
"Show me the IC. That's your confidence level."
Hindsight Bias
Believing past events were predictable after already knowing the outcome.
MEDIUM tap to flip →
In Trading
"I knew that was going to happen." Journal entries written to explain a known outcome — not real-time reasoning. Prevents accurate learning.
FINN Counter
Write journal entries before you know the outcome: hypothesis, setup, criteria, stop, target. Post-trade: outcome vs. hypothesis.
"Journal entry goes in before the trade closes."
Pre-Trade & Post-Trade Psychological Check
Pre-Trade Check — Before Every Entry
  • Emotional stateRate 1–5: calm (1) → activated (5). Above 3 after a loss? Do not enter.
  • Setup validityDoes this meet all written entry criteria — or am I forcing a trade because I want to be in?
  • Risk definedStop level identified. Position size calculated. Dollar risk known before the click.
  • Target definedFirst profit target set. R/R ratio confirmed — at least 2:1?
  • Confirmation checkWhat is the best argument against this trade? Have I actively considered it?
  • Daily loss statusWithin daily loss limit? At 80%, reduce size by half. At 100%, stop trading.
Post-Trade Check — After Every Close
  • Outcome vs. hypothesisWas the hypothesis correct? Was the outcome correct? These are separate questions — a good process can produce a bad outcome.
  • Rule adherenceFollowed entry, stop, and target rules exactly? If not — what deviated, why, and what was the P&L impact?
  • Emotional logEmotional state at entry? At exit? Did emotion influence any decision?
  • Signal assessmentWas the IC of this signal positive in the 30 days prior? Good signal or lucky outcome?
  • One learningOne specific, actionable thing learned from this trade — win or loss.
  • Tomorrow's readinessAm I in a state to trade tomorrow calmly? If no — what would need to change?
FINN Psychological Standards
Rule 01
The daily loss limit is not a guideline. It is a circuit breaker. When it trips, trading stops — not slows, stops. No exceptions. The rule exists for the moment when you feel most certain it doesn't apply.
Rule 02
Wrong predictions are required. A trader who only logs correct predictions is training on corrupted data. Document every hypothesis — especially the ones that fail. That is where the actual learning lives.
Rule 03
The journal is more valuable than the P&L. P&L is the outcome of luck + skill. The journal is the record of your decision process. Improving the journal is how edge is built over time.
Rule 04
Deviations from rules are data, not failures — but they must be logged and analyzed. "The setup looked different" is the beginning of a journal entry, not the end. Document the specific reason, outcome, and implication.
Rule 05
Emotional confidence and analytical confidence are not the same thing. Feeling certain is not evidence. The IC score is evidence. The backtest is evidence. The feeling is a data point about your emotional state — nothing more.
Rule 06
The plan was written when you were thinking clearly. Honor it. The moment of entry or exit is the moment of maximum emotional distortion. Trust the calmer, more analytical version of you who wrote the plan before the trade.

"The market does not care about your feelings. It will not wait for you to calm down. It will not give back what you lost because you deserve it. The only variable the market cannot take from you is the quality of your decision process. Protect that. The P&L will take care of itself."
