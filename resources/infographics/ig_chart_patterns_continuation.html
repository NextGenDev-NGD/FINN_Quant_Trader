What Continuation Patterns Are

Continuation patterns form when a strong trend pauses to consolidate before resuming. The consolidation represents a temporary equilibrium — traders taking profits, late buyers hesitating — before the dominant force (buyers in an uptrend, sellers in a downtrend) reasserts control. The pattern identifies the pause; the breakout confirms the continuation.

Key rule: All continuation patterns require a strong prior trend to be valid. A flag forming in the middle of a sideways range is not a flag — it is just chop. Confirm the prior trend before naming the pattern.

Prior trend:Strong uptrend (flagpole)

Duration:5–20 candles (days); longer = weaker

Duration:5–20 candles; declining volume into the flag
Breakout:Close below lower flag boundary on above-average volume
Target:Flagpole height subtracted from breakout point

Rising Wedge / Falling Wedge
Context-Dependent 
 Rising Wedge (bearish):
 ╱══╗  ← Highs rising
╱═══╝     Lows rising faster
           Converging upward
 ↓ Breaks DOWN (bearish)

 Falling Wedge (bullish):
 ╲═══╗  ← Highs falling faster
  ╲══╝    Lows falling
            Converging downward
 ↑ Breaks UP (bullish)
Rising wedge:Both highs and lows rising BUT converging. Buyers losing strength. Breaks bearish.
Falling wedge:Both highs and lows falling BUT converging. Sellers losing strength. Breaks bullish.
Key tell:Lines must converge. Parallel lines = channel, not wedge.
Duration:Typically 10–40 candles — longer than flags/pennants
Volume:Declining throughout; expands significantly on breakout
Triangles
Ascending Triangle
Bullish Continuation 
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ ← Flat resistance
 *   *   *
  \ / \ / \
   ╱     ╱  ← Rising lows
 ╱     ╱
↑ BREAKOUT above flat resistance
Structure:Flat resistance line (same high tested multiple times) + rising lower trendline
Meaning:Buyers are increasingly aggressive (higher lows) but sellers defend the same level — until they're exhausted
Breakout:Close above flat resistance on volume. Each test of resistance depletes sell orders.
Target:Height of triangle at its widest point added to breakout level
Touches:Minimum 2 on each line before valid

Symmetrical Triangle
Direction of Prior Trend 
╲           ╱
 ╲         ╱  ← Both lines converge
  ╲       ╱     Lower highs
   ╲     ╱      Higher lows
    ╲   ╱
     ╲ ╱
      ↕ Breakout in trend direction
Structure:Lower highs + higher lows — both lines converge to an apex
Neutral:Neither side is winning. Direction of prior trend usually predicts breakout direction — but not always.
Breakout timing:Typically occurs at 60–75% of the way to the apex. Past the apex = pattern failure.
Volume:Must expand significantly on the breakout — more critical here than with directional triangles
Rectangle (Trading Range)
Direction of Prior Trend 
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ ← Flat resistance
 *   *   *   *
* * * * * * * *
 *   *   *   *
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ ← Flat support
↑ or ↓ Breakout in trend direction
Structure:Price oscillates between flat resistance and flat support — a defined horizontal channel
Meaning:Supply and demand are in temporary equilibrium. The prior trend determines which side eventually wins.
Duration:Can last weeks to months. More touches = stronger the eventual breakout.
Breakout:Close through either boundary on above-average volume. False breaks are common — wait for close confirmation.

Volume is the critical confirming variable for every continuation pattern. A breakout on below-average volume is a warning sign, not a signal. The ideal breakout candle has volume at least 1.5× the 20-period average. Without it, treat the breakout as suspect until volume confirms.

The target is a starting point, not a guarantee. The measured-move target (flagpole height, triangle height) tells you where price would reach if the pattern plays out perfectly. It does not tell you it will get there. Manage the trade dynamically — tighten stops as price approaches the target, do not count on hitting it exactly.

Prior trend is mandatory context. A bullish continuation pattern without a prior uptrend is not a bullish pattern. Always confirm what came before before naming the pattern.
