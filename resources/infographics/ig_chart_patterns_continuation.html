Continuation patterns form when a strong trend pauses to consolidate before resuming. The consolidation is a temporary equilibrium — traders taking profits, late buyers hesitating — before the dominant force reasserts. A strong prior trend is mandatory context. A flag forming in a sideways range is not a flag — it is chop. Confirm the prior trend before naming the pattern. Click any card to expand breakout conditions, target calculation, and failure mode.

Bull Flag
Bullish Cont.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Prior Trend Required
Strong uptrend (the flagpole). Sharp, near-vertical move before the consolidation.
Breakout Signal
Close above upper flag boundary on above-average volume. Ideal: volume ≥ 1.5× the 20-period average on the breakout candle.
Target Calculation
Flagpole height added to the breakout point. Duration: 5–20 candles — longer = weaker.
Failure Mode
Close below the lower flag boundary — especially on expanding volume. The pattern has failed; the prior trend may be over.
"Declining volume into the flag is a feature, not a bug. Breakout volume is the confirmation."

Bear Flag
Bearish Cont.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Prior Trend Required
Strong downtrend (the flagpole). Sharp, near-vertical move down before consolidation drifts slightly upward.
Breakout Signal
Close below lower flag boundary on above-average volume. Mirror image of bull flag logic.
Target Calculation
Flagpole height subtracted from the breakout point. Same 5–20 candle duration rule.
Failure Mode
Close above the upper flag boundary on volume — sellers have lost control. The downtrend may be reversing.
"Upward drift on low volume inside a downtrend = short-side setup building, not reversal."

Pennant
Prior Trend Dir.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
vs. Flag
Pennant has converging lines (mini-triangle shape) while a flag has parallel lines. Both follow a sharp flagpole move.
Breakout Signal
In the direction of the prior trend. Volume declines sharply into the apex; must expand on the breakout candle.
Target Calculation
Flagpole height added to (bull) or subtracted from (bear) the breakout point. Duration: 5–15 candles.
Failure Mode
Price reaches the apex without breaking — the pattern has dissolved. Also: breakout in the opposite direction of the prior trend.
"Tighter consolidation than a flag. Breakout volume is even more critical here."

Rising / Falling Wedge
Rising = BearishFalling = Bullish
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Key Tell
Lines must converge — parallel lines = channel, not wedge. Rising wedge: buyers losing momentum as lines squeeze upward. Falling wedge: sellers losing momentum as lines squeeze downward.
Breakout Signal
Rising wedge breaks down; falling wedge breaks up. Volume declining throughout, then spiking on breakout — this confirmation is critical.
Target Calculation
Height of the wedge at its widest point applied to the breakout level. Duration: 10–40 candles.
Failure Mode
Rising wedge breaks up (rare, aggressive bullish continuation). Falling wedge breaks down. In both cases, the measured move from the wedge height becomes the failure target in the opposite direction.
"Counter-intuitive: rising wedge = bearish. Falling wedge = bullish. The convergence is the tell, not the direction."

Ascending Triangle
Bullish Cont.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Structure
Flat resistance line (same high tested multiple times) + rising lower trendline. Buyers increasingly aggressive, sellers defend the same level — until exhausted.
Breakout Signal
Close above flat resistance on above-average volume. Each test depletes sell orders at the level — eventually the bids overwhelm.
Target Calculation
Height of triangle at its widest point added to the breakout level. Minimum 2 touches on each line.
Failure Mode
Close below the rising lower trendline — buyers are no longer defending higher lows. Pattern invalidated; can reverse sharply.
"The higher lows are buyers getting more aggressive. The flat resistance is sellers running out of ammunition."

Descending Triangle
Bearish Cont.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Structure
Flat support line + falling upper trendline (lower highs). Sellers increasingly aggressive, buyers defend same floor — until exhausted.
Breakout Signal
Close below flat support on above-average volume. Each test depletes buy orders — eventually sellers overwhelm.
Target Calculation
Height of triangle at widest point subtracted from breakout level. Minimum 2 touches on each line.
Failure Mode
Close above the falling upper trendline — sellers are no longer making lower highs. Pattern fails; potential sharp reversal upward.
"Mirror of the ascending. Lower highs = sellers tightening. Each support test depletes the buy-side."

Symmetrical Triangle
Prior Trend Dir.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Structure
Lower highs + higher lows — both lines converge. Neither side is winning. Direction of prior trend usually predicts breakout direction — but not always.
Breakout Signal
Typically at 60–75% of the way to apex. Volume expansion is more critical here than with directional triangles — the neutral structure means volume must confirm direction.
Target Calculation
Height at widest point applied to breakout level. Past the apex = pattern failure.
Failure Mode
Breakout in the wrong direction (against prior trend) on high volume. Or: price reaches the apex without breaking (indecision pattern dissolution).
"Breakout volume is everything here. Low volume break from a symmetrical triangle is a trap."

Rectangle (Trading Range)
Prior Trend Dir.
Breakout · Target · Failure Mode ▾
Structure
Price oscillates between flat resistance and flat support — a defined horizontal channel. Supply and demand in temporary equilibrium.
Breakout Signal
Close through either boundary on above-average volume. False breaks are common — always wait for a full candle close outside the boundary.
Target Calculation
Height of the rectangle applied to the breakout level. More touches = stronger the eventual breakout. Duration: weeks to months.
Failure Mode
Multiple false breaks in both directions — the rectangle is dissolving rather than resolving. Step aside and wait for a clear directional close with volume.
"False breaks from rectangles are common. The rule: close confirmation only. A wick through the boundary is not a breakout."

Volume is the critical confirming variable. A breakout on below-average volume is a warning sign, not a signal. The ideal breakout candle has volume at least 1.5× the 20-period average. Without it, treat the breakout as suspect until a second close confirms.

The target is a starting point, not a guarantee. The measured-move target (flagpole height, triangle height) tells you where price would reach if the pattern plays out fully. Manage the trade dynamically — tighten stops as price approaches target. Do not count on hitting it exactly.

Prior trend is mandatory context. A bullish continuation pattern without a prior uptrend is not a bullish pattern. Confirm what came before before naming the pattern.

"The pattern tells you what the market is doing. The volume tells you if it means it. A breakout with volume is a hypothesis worth testing. A breakout without volume is a trap worth avoiding. Run the IC before you systematize any of these — what looks obvious on a chart may have an IC of 0.01."
