Confirmation Bias
Seeking out information that supports your existing view while ignoring evidence that contradicts it.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
You're bullish on a stock. You read 5 bullish articles and dismiss the one bearish analyst. You hold through warning signs because you're curating your information to fit your thesis.
FINN Countermeasure
Write the bear case before entering. Assign a specific IC to the signal — not to the story. The IC doesn't care about your thesis.
"What would make me wrong? Write it down before you enter."

Recency Bias
Overweighting recent events and assuming they are more representative of the future than they actually are.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
After a 3-week bull run, you size up assuming the trend continues forever. After 3 straight losses, you size down to near-zero convinced you've lost your edge permanently.
FINN Countermeasure
Evaluate edge over at least 20–30 trades, not 3–5. Size consistently based on pre-defined rules, not on recent P&L. The IC is calculated on historical data, not last week's results.
"Three losses is not a sample. It is three data points."

Loss Aversion
Losses feel approximately 2× more painful than equivalent gains feel good. Pain of losing $100 ≠ pleasure of making $100.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
You take profits too early on winners (relieving the fear of giving them back) and hold losers too long (refusing to accept the loss as real). Net effect: small wins, large losses.
FINN Countermeasure
Pre-define exit levels before entry. The plan was made when you were rational — honor it when you're emotional. Use the R-multiple to measure trades, not dollar P&L.
"The stop was placed at invalidation. If price is there, the thesis is wrong."

Sunk Cost Fallacy
Continuing to hold (or add to) a position because of what you've already lost in it, not because of its current expected value.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
"I'm down 20% — I can't sell now, I need to get back to breakeven." The original capital is gone regardless of what you do next. Your decision should be based on what the position will do from here, not from entry.
FINN Countermeasure
Ask: "If I had no position in this stock right now, would I buy it at today's price?" If no, the only rational action is to exit. The past loss is irrelevant to the current decision.
"The market does not know what you paid. It does not care."

Anchoring Bias
Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making decisions, even when that information is irrelevant.
MEDIUM RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
You bought a stock at $80. It's at $60. You keep targeting $80 as your "get out" level — anchored to your cost basis rather than what the chart says is resistance. Or: you're anchored to last year's P/E and miss that fundamentals changed.
FINN Countermeasure
Define price targets from the current chart structure, not from where you bought. Your entry price is not support. The market did not draw that level — you did.
"Set targets from the structure. Not from where you got in."

Overconfidence Bias
Overestimating your own ability to predict market outcomes, assess risks, or outperform — especially after a winning streak.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
After 5 winning trades, you increase size to 2× and skip the context check because you're "in the zone." One bad trade at 2× wipes out all 5 wins. Overconfidence is the primary driver of blowup accounts.
FINN Countermeasure
Size is defined by the risk rules, not by your win streak. After 5 winners, ask: "Is my position sizing the same as it was on trade 1?" If not, you are no longer trading your system.
"Show me the IC. Not the streak."

Hindsight Bias
Believing, after the fact, that an outcome was predictable or obvious — even when it wasn't at the time of the decision.
MEDIUM RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
"Of course it crashed — the chart was so obvious." You selectively remember only the signals that predicted the move, not the 15 other signals that gave the same reading but didn't result in a move. This inflates your perceived skill.
FINN Countermeasure
Review trades using only the information you had at the time of entry. The journal records the pre-trade rationale. Judge the decision quality, not the outcome quality. Right process, wrong outcome = still a good trade.
"Judge the decision, not the outcome. They are not the same thing."

Disposition Effect
The tendency to sell winning positions too early and hold losing positions too long — the inverse of what edge-based trading requires.
HIGH RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
You exit winners at +5% because "a profit is a profit." You hold losers at −15% hoping they'll come back. After 20 trades: average win = $150, average loss = $600. Negative expectancy despite a 60% win rate.
FINN Countermeasure
Target and stop are set before entry and honored after entry. Wins are held until target or trailing stop. Losses are cut at the predefined stop. Calculate the R-multiple on every trade — the disposition effect shows up in the data immediately.
"Cut losers fast. Let winners run. The math requires both."

Gambler's Fallacy
Believing that past random outcomes influence future ones — that after a losing streak, a win is "due," or that a hot signal must cool off.
MEDIUM RISK tap to flip →
In Trading
"I've had 4 losses in a row, so the next trade must win — I'll size up." Each trade is independent. The market has no memory of your prior trades. Increasing size after a losing streak based on "I'm due" is not risk management — it's gambling logic applied to trading.
FINN Countermeasure
Edge is expressed over large samples. A system with 55% win rate can produce 6 consecutive losses naturally. Treat each trade as independent. Size to the rule, not to the recent sequence.
"The next trade does not know about the last five. Neither should your position size."

Monthly Bias Audit — 9 Questions to Ask Yourself
01Did I hold any position past its stop because I believed it would recover?
02Did I exit a winner early because I was afraid of giving the gain back?
03Did I size up after a winning streak without a rule that justified it?
04Did I look for confirming evidence for a thesis I already held?
05Did I set an exit target based on my entry price rather than the chart structure?
06After a losing streak, did I change my size based on being "due" for a win?
07Did I judge any trade as "obvious in hindsight" without checking my pre-trade rationale?
08Did I continue holding a position primarily because of what I had already lost in it?
09Did I overestimate my ability to predict a specific outcome based on recent success?
Biases aren't flaws. They're evolved heuristics that worked well for human survival and work very poorly in financial markets. The countermeasure isn't willpower — it's rules. A written stop order doesn't negotiate with loss aversion. A pre-defined target doesn't bow to overconfidence. Systems are the answer. The log is how you catch what slips through.
