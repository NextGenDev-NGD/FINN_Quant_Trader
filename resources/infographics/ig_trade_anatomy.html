1
Signal Identified
Something triggered your attention — define what
▼
What to do
Write down the specific signal: pattern name, indicator level, or rule trigger. "Stock looked interesting" is not a signal. "Bullish engulfing on daily at 200d SMA support, IC = 0.07 on this setup in backtest" is a signal.
FINN Check
What does the backtest say? What is the IC on this exact setup type, in this regime, on this ticker? If you don't know, you don't have a signal — you have an impulse.
Common Mistake
Acting on "feel" without a defined signal. If you can't write a one-sentence rule that would generate the same signal mechanically, you're not trading a system.
2
Context Check
Regime, structure, volume — does the environment support the signal?
▼
What to check
  • Market regime: bull / bear / choppy (is this the right signal type for the regime?)
  • Price structure: is the signal in the direction of the larger trend?
  • Volume: above or below average on the signal candle?
  • Key levels: is there a major S/R level within the stop distance?
  • Upcoming catalysts: earnings, Fed, macro event within hold period?
FINN Check
Is the regime correct for this signal type? A momentum signal in a choppy market has near-zero IC. If the context doesn't support the signal, don't trade it — wait for alignment.
3
Size Calculated
Position size = dollar risk ÷ stop distance. Always.
▼
The Formula
Dollar Risk = Account × 2%
Stop Distance = |Entry − Stop|
Shares = Dollar Risk ÷ Stop Distance
Verify: Position Value ≤ 20% of Account
FINN Check
What is your dollar risk on this trade? Say the number out loud before placing the order. If losing this amount would affect your emotional state, reduce size until it doesn't.
Common Mistake
Sizing to a dollar amount ("I'll buy $5,000 worth") instead of to a risk amount. This is position-based, not risk-based sizing — it violates Rule 1 and leads to inconsistent exposure across trades.
4
Order Placed
Entry type, stop order, and target set before you click
▼
Order Sequence
  • Entry: limit order at the signal level (not market — control slippage)
  • Stop: stop-limit order placed immediately after fill
  • Target: limit sell order at calculated target (2:1+ R:R minimum)
  • Confirm all three orders are live before walking away
FINN Check
Are all three orders — entry, stop, and target — in the system before the trade is live? A trade without a stop is not a trade. It's a gamble with no exit plan.
5
Trade Managed
Move stop to breakeven at 1:1 — then let it run
▼
Management Rules
  • At 1:1 R:R (price moved one risk unit in your favor): move stop to breakeven
  • At 1.5:1: trail stop using 1× ATR below most recent swing low
  • At 2:1: take partial profit (⅓ position) if pre-planned
  • Do not add to losing positions. Ever.
FINN Check
Is your stop protecting capital or protecting ego? Moving a stop further out to avoid being stopped is not management — it's risk expansion without additional edge.
Common Mistake
Moving stop further away when price moves against you. The stop was placed at the invalidation level. If price reaches it, the thesis is wrong. Honor the original plan.
6
Exit Executed
Target hit, stop hit, or rule-based exit — all three are valid
▼
Exit Types
Target hit: pre-planned exit fires. Best outcome — plan worked. Don't second-guess and hold for more.

Stop hit: thesis was wrong. Get out clean. Do not convert to an investment.

Rule-based: time stop (held past max hold period), regime change, or catalyst risk ahead of earnings.
FINN Check
Did you exit at the planned level or did you move the target? Moving a target higher because a trade is working is fine — if you have a rule for it. Improvising at the exit is not management.
7
Trade Logged
Every trade. Entry, exit, size, rationale, outcome, emotional state.
▼
Required Journal Fields
  • Ticker, date, direction (long/short)
  • Signal: exact rule that triggered the trade
  • Entry price, stop price, target price
  • Position size (shares) and dollar risk
  • Exit price, P&L, R-multiple (actual / planned)
  • Pre-trade emotional state (1–5 scale)
  • What did the data say? What would change your mind?
FINN Check
No log = no feedback loop = no edge development. You cannot improve a process you're not measuring. The log is not optional. It is the mechanism that converts experience into edge.
✓ Target Hit
Plan worked. Exit at the pre-planned level. Log the win, review the setup quality. Resist the urge to hold for more — discipline in winners builds the habit that protects in losers.
✗ Stop Hit
Thesis was wrong. Exit clean. A loss at the planned stop is a correctly executed trade — the signal had negative IC on this instance. Log the loss and move to the next trade.
→ Rule-Based Exit
Regime changed, hold period expired, or catalyst risk emerged. Exit not because of price, but because the original context is no longer valid. Log the reason — these exits generate the most useful data.
Most traders spend 90% of their time on signal generation and 10% on execution and logging. FINN spends it the other way. The signal is a starting point. The execution, management, and log are where edge is built or destroyed.
