Risk management is not what you do after a trade goes wrong. It is what determines your position size, your stop placement, and your daily exposure limit before you enter a single trade.

All 6 rules apply on every trade, every day, without exception. There is no "this setup is so good I'll skip rule 3." That thinking is how accounts are blown.
The 6 Rules — click any card to expand the rationale
1
2% Max Risk Per Trade
Never risk more than 2% of total account on any single position
Hard Limit
Risk ≤ 2% of account/trade
Position size = (Account × 0.02) ÷ (Entry − Stop). This calculation is non-negotiable. A conviction narrative is not a risk override.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
At 2% risk, you can lose 50 consecutive trades and still have 36% of your capital. At 10% risk, 10 consecutive losses = 65% loss. Recovery from −65% requires a +186% return. The math is asymmetric and unforgiving. Ruin is permanent; edge is not.
Violation Pattern
"This setup is different" → sizing up to 5–10% of account on a strong conviction trade.
How it ends: one gap-down wipes out 20+ winning trades. Account permanently impaired.
2
6% Daily Drawdown Limit
Stop trading for the day if total losses reach 6% of account
Daily Kill Switch
Stop if daily P&L ≤ −6%
When you hit −6% on the day, close all positions and stop trading. No revenge trades. The market will be open tomorrow; your capital may not be.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
Bad days cluster. When you've lost three times in a row, your emotional state is impaired — fear and anger degrade decision quality. Continuing to trade after hitting the limit compounds emotional decision-making with financial damage. A forced break protects capital from revenge trading when you're most vulnerable to it.
Violation Pattern
After 3 losing trades: "I need to make it back today" → taking a 4th, 5th, 6th trade.
How it ends: emotional state compounds losses. −6% becomes −15% in a single session.
3
ATR-Based Stop Placement
Stops set using ATR — not round numbers, not support lines alone
Stop Formula
Stop = Entry − (1.5 × ATR14)
Place stops 1.5–2× ATR beyond the entry. Accounts for normal price noise without giving up too much per trade. Never use round numbers as stop levels.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
Round-number stops are visible to everyone and get hunted deliberately by market makers and algorithmic traders. ATR-based stops are calibrated to actual price volatility, not arbitrary chart levels — this reduces the probability of stop-hunt wipeouts before a trade has time to develop.
Violation Pattern
Placing a stop at the obvious swing low or at a round number like $50.00 or $100.00.
How it ends: stop hunted. Price dips to $49.95, fills the stop, then reverses to the original target.
4
Minimum 2:1 R:R Required
Do not enter any trade with risk-to-reward ratio below 2:1
Entry Gate
Target ≥ Entry + (2 × Risk)
Calculate target and stop before entering. If Target / Risk < 2.0, skip the trade. A great setup with a 1:1 R:R is still a bad trade.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
With a 50% win rate and 2:1 R:R, you have positive expectancy. At 1:1, you need >50% win rate just to break even after commissions — and most strategies don't sustain >50% win rates. The 2:1 minimum creates a structural edge that survives mediocre win rates. Do the math before every trade, every time.
Violation Pattern
Entering a 1:1 trade because "it's a sure thing" or "the setup looks perfect."
How it ends: even a 60% win rate at 1:1 R:R barely covers commissions. Slow, invisible account bleed.
5
Max 20% Sector Concentration
No more than 20% of capital in any single sector at any time
Concentration Limit
Sector exposure ≤ 20%
Track sector allocation across all open positions. Technology, healthcare, energy — no single sector should represent more than 20% of total deployed capital.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
Sector shocks — earnings surprises, regulatory announcements, macro data — hit correlated stocks simultaneously. Within a sector, correlations spike to 0.8–0.95 during stress events. Your diversification between tickers in the same sector is essentially zero when it matters most.
Violation Pattern
Running 4 tech trades simultaneously during earnings season.
How it ends: one sector rotation or macro event hits all 4 at once. Correlation = full loss on the entire position block.
6
Document Every Trade
No trade exists if it is not logged before and after execution
Documentation Standard
Log entry + exit + rationale
Before entry: record thesis, entry, stop, target, position size. After exit: record outcome, what was right, what was wrong, what to do differently.
▼ See rationale + violation
Why This Rule Exists
Undocumented trades produce no learning. The log is not paperwork — it is the feedback loop that separates a trader who improves from one who repeats the same mistake with different tickers. Without pre-trade documentation, you cannot distinguish skill from luck when the trade closes.
Violation Pattern
"I'll log it later" → never logging it → repeating the same mistake next week.
How it ends: no feedback loop. Same biases, same mistakes, indefinitely. Improvement is impossible without data.
Account-Level Kill Switch
⏹
Stop Trading Immediately If Any Condition Is Met No exceptions
  • Daily loss exceeds 6% of account (Rule 2 trigger — stop for the day)
  • Monthly drawdown exceeds 15% of account start value (stop for the month — return to backtesting)
  • Live IC drops below 0.03 for 2 consecutive months (signal decay — rebuild the signal)
  • You take a trade without calculating position size first
  • You deviate from your entry rules during an emotional state (angry, scared, euphoric)

"Risk management is not about preventing losses — you will have losses. It is about making sure that a string of losses cannot end your ability to trade. Every rule above exists because someone, at some point, broke it and paid with their account. The rules are not constraints on your edge. They are what keeps you in the game long enough for your edge to compound."
