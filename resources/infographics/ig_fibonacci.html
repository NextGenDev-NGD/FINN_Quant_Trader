How to Draw — 4-Step Protocol
01
Identify the significant swing
Find a clear, impulsive move — a strong rally (uptrend) or strong decline (downtrend). The move should be obvious on your chart without zooming in. The bigger and cleaner the swing, the more reliable the levels.
02
Anchor at swing extremes
Place the tool at swing low → high for uptrend retracements. At swing high → low for downtrend. Use wick extremes — not candle bodies — as anchor points. This sets the 0% and 100% levels.
03
Wait for price to retrace
After anchoring, wait — do not trade the level until price reaches it AND shows a reaction. A level alone is not an entry. Look for: candlestick reversal pattern at the level, volume spike, momentum divergence.
04
Require confluence
The best Fibonacci entries occur when multiple signals converge: Fib level + prior S/R + moving average + volume. Each additional signal raises probability. One signal alone = noise.
FINN Rules — Fibonacci
61.8% is the highest-probability level
The golden ratio level is where institutional algo orders cluster. If price reaches 61.8% and holds, it is often a high-quality entry in the direction of the prior trend. Treat it as your primary watch level.
Confluence makes the trade
A Fibonacci level by itself is not an edge. Fib level + S&R zone + candlestick signal + volume reaction = tradeable confluence. Stack the signals before entering.
Extensions tell you where to exit
Use extensions (127.2%, 161.8%, 200%) as profit targets, not entries. When a retracement entry works, these levels give you data-backed exits. 161.8% is the primary target; scale out in thirds.
Don't anchor to noise swings
Fibonacci levels are only as reliable as the swing you anchor them to. A valid swing is impulsive, clearly directional, and visible on your primary timeframe without excessive zoom.
Timeframe determines strength
A 61.8% on a weekly chart is more powerful than a 61.8% on a 5-minute chart. Draw Fibonacci on your analysis timeframe (weekly or daily) and execute entries on a lower timeframe for precision.
Invalidate cleanly at 100%
If price closes through the 100% level — below the swing low in an uptrend — the retracement has become a reversal. Exit the trade. Close beyond 100% is a hard invalidation. Don't rationalize.
Confluence Combos — What to Stack

Fib + Support / Resistance

If a prior S&R zone lines up within 0.5% of a Fibonacci level, the zone is doubly validated. Both institutional buyers (algo Fib orders) and manual traders (S&R) are watching the same price.
Fib level + Prior S/R zone = High-priority watch
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
When a Fib level and a prior S&R zone overlap within 0.5%, you have two independent reasons for the same price to matter. That's not a coincidence — both were formed by price behavior. Every participant who bought at the prior support AND every algo watching the 61.8% is now defending the same level. That's your trade.

Fib + Moving Average

A Fibonacci level that aligns with the 20-EMA, 50-SMA, or 200-SMA creates strong dynamic support. Price often bounces off the Fib zone because the MA reinforces it simultaneously.
Fib level + 20/50/200 MA = Dynamic confluence
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The moving average is dynamic — it moves with price. When a static Fib level and a dynamic MA arrive at the same price at the same time, the probability is compounding. Two completely different calculation methods producing the same support zone. Log this setup every time it appears and track the IC. What does the data say?

Fib + Volume Reaction

When price reaches a Fib level and volume dries up (exhaustion of selling in an uptrend), then spikes as price bounces — this is the cleanest signal that institutional demand is present at that exact level.
Fib level reached + Volume dries + Volume spike on bounce = Entry signal
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The volume dry-up IS the signal that sellers are exhausted at this level. The volume spike on the bounce confirms that buyers stepped in at the same level. This is the sequence: level → dry-up → spike → entry. Three steps, not one. Missing the dry-up step means entering before the signal, not with it.

Fib + Candlestick Pattern

A hammer, bullish engulfing, or doji at a Fibonacci level provides visual confirmation that a reversal is occurring at that precise price. This is the most direct entry timing signal.
Fib level + Hammer / Engulfing = Triggered entry
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The Fibonacci level tells you where to watch. The candlestick tells you when to act. Without the candle, you're anticipating. Without the Fib, you're entering anywhere. Together: a price level with a mechanism. The candle is the trigger — don't enter before it forms.

Python — Fibonacci Calculator
import yfinance as yf import pandas as pd def fib_levels(ticker, period="6mo"): df = yf.download(ticker, period=period, progress=False) df.columns = [c[0] if isinstance(c, tuple) else c for c in df.columns] swing_high = df['High'].max() swing_low = df['Low'].min() diff = swing_high - swing_low # Retracement levels (from high, pulling back toward low) levels = { '0.0% (High)' : swing_high, '23.6%' : swing_high - 0.236 * diff, '38.2%' : swing_high - 0.382 * diff, '50.0%' : swing_high - 0.500 * diff, '61.8% ★ Golden': swing_high - 0.618 * diff, '78.6%' : swing_high - 0.786 * diff, '100% (Low)' : swing_low, '127.2% Ext' : swing_low - 0.272 * diff, '161.8% Ext ★' : swing_low - 0.618 * diff, } print(f"\n=== Fibonacci Levels: {ticker} ===") print(f"Swing High: ${swing_high:.2f} | Swing Low: ${swing_low:.2f}\n") for label, price in levels.items(): print(f" {label:<20} ${price:.2f}") return pd.Series(levels, name=ticker) # Example usage fib_levels('SPY')
