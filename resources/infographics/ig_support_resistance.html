Core Definitions
Support
A price level where buying interest is strong enough to halt or reverse a decline. Why it works: traders remember prior lows and place buy orders there. The level holds because enough participants act on it simultaneously.
Think of it as a floor the market has tested and respected. The more times it has been tested and held, the stronger the floor.
Resistance
A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to halt or reverse an advance. Why it works: traders who bought below and are sitting on gains sell at prior highs. Traders who bought at the prior high and held through a loss sell to break even.
Think of it as a ceiling. Each test that fails reinforces it.
Zone, Not Line
Support and resistance are zones, not precise lines. Price rarely turns at exactly $47.50 — it turns somewhere between $47.00 and $48.00.
Draw your levels as rectangles 0.5–1% wide. A wick through the zone that recovers is not an invalidation. A candle close through the zone on volume is.

How to Identify and Draw S&R Zones
1
Start on the Daily Chart
Always identify major S&R on the daily timeframe first. Daily levels are where institutional players make decisions. Intraday levels exist within the context of daily levels.
Look for: price highs and lows tested multiple times, areas where price consolidated for multiple sessions, prior breakout levels.
2
Find the Touches
A level requires at least 2 touches to be meaningful. A single high or low is a data point. Two or more touches at approximately the same price is a zone.
More touches = stronger zone — but after 4–5 tests, a zone often breaks. Each test depletes the limit orders waiting at that level.
3
Draw Zones, Not Lines
Place the zone to capture the bodies of the candles, not the wicks. Wicks represent temporary excursions. Bodies represent where price settled — where buyers and sellers agreed.
Zone width: typically the range of the bodies across all touches. Usually 0.5–1.5% wide on liquid equities.
Zone Strength Factors
Factor Strengthens the Zone Weakens the Zone
Number of touches 3+ touches with clean bounces 1 touch; untested level
Volume at the touches Above-average volume at each bounce — institutional activity Low-volume tests — thin order book
Time spent at the level Extended consolidation near the zone — multiple sessions Brief, fast touches — price moved through quickly
Recency Tested within the last 20–40 trading days Level from 6+ months ago — order book has changed
Timeframe of origin Weekly or daily chart level Only visible on a 5-minute chart
Round numbers $50, $100, $200 — attract more orders by default Arbitrary prices ($47.23) — no psychological anchor
Test · Retest · Invalidation
Test
Price approaches a known level. A successful test: price touches the zone and reverses — the zone holds. Buying or selling pressure at the level was sufficient.
Watch for: a reversal candle (hammer, engulfing) forming at the zone with above-average volume.
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
A test is not an entry — it is the beginning of the question. Price touched the zone. Now: what is the candle doing? Is volume confirming? Are you above or below a key moving average? The zone told you where to look. The candle and volume tell you whether to act.
Retest
After a breakout above resistance, price pulls back to the former resistance zone and holds. The broken resistance now acts as support — this is the polarity flip in action.
This retest is often the best entry point for breakout traders. Why: traders who missed the breakout buy the retest. Former sellers are now buyers.
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The retest is where breakout traders get their second chance — and often the cleaner entry. The breakout candle was high-risk (spread wide, stops unclear). The retest candle is low-risk: defined entry, tight stop below the zone, measured target. If the retest fails on high volume, the breakout was a fake. Document it.
Invalidation
A support zone is invalidated when price closes — not just wicks — decisively below it on above-average volume. The orders at that level have been exhausted.
One wick through a zone is not invalidation. A closing candle body through the zone on volume is.
FINN Analysis ▼
FINN Check
The most expensive word in trading is "but." "It broke support, but it was so strong before." Once a support zone closes below on high volume — the market has spoken. Update your levels. The anchoring bias of a "strong" prior level is how traders hold losers too long. The market does not care what the level was. Only where it is now.
Role Reversal (Polarity Flip)
Resistance → Support
When price breaks above a resistance zone and pulls back, the former resistance becomes support. The logic: traders who sold at resistance and were wrong are now buyers at the same level. Traders who missed the breakout buy the dip to the former resistance.
Entry: Pull back to former resistance + bullish reversal candle + volume confirmation
Support → Resistance
When price breaks below a support zone, the former support becomes resistance. Traders who bought at support and held through the breakdown will sell to break even when price returns to their entry level.
Entry: Bounce to former support zone + bearish reversal candle + volume confirmation
⬡ FINN
"The most common S&R mistake is treating a level as valid after it has been cleanly broken. Once a support zone closes below on high volume — it is no longer support. Relabeling it as support because 'it was strong before' is anchoring bias. The market has spoken. Update your levels. What you believed about a level last week has no bearing on what the market is doing with it today."
— FINN: S&R is context, not signal.
FINN Application Rules
Context, Not Signal
A support zone tells you where a reaction may occur. Your candlestick pattern and volume tell you whether to act. S&R alone is never an entry — it is the starting point for the question.
Confluence Is the Multiplier
A Fibonacci 61.8% level + a prior daily high + a 50-day moving average in the same area = a high-confluence zone. One S&R line in isolation = one data point. Stack the evidence.
Close Confirmation Only
Wicks through levels are not breaks. A closing candle body through the zone on volume is a break. Do not react to intrabar movement — wait for the close before updating your levels.
More Tests = Weaker Zone Ahead
Each test depletes limit orders sitting at the level. A zone tested 5+ times is close to breaking — the order book has been absorbed. High test count is not strength; it is depletion.
