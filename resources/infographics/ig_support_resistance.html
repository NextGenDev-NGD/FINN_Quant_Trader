Core Definitions
Support

A price level where buying interest is strong enough to halt or reverse a decline. Why it works: traders remember prior lows and place buy orders there. The level holds because enough participants act on it simultaneously.

Think of it as a floor the market has tested and respected. The more times it has been tested and held, the stronger the floor.

Resistance

A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to halt or reverse an advance. Why it works: traders who bought below and are sitting on gains sell at prior highs. Traders who bought at the prior high and held through a loss sell to break even.

Think of it as a ceiling. Each test that fails reinforces it.

Zone, Not Line

Support and resistance are zones, not precise lines. Price rarely turns at exactly $47.50 — it turns somewhere between $47.00 and $48.00.

Draw your levels as rectangles 0.5–1% wide. A wick through the zone that recovers is not an invalidation. A candle close through the zone is.

How to Identify and Draw S&R Zones
Step 1 — Start on the Daily Chart

Always identify major S&R on the daily timeframe first. Daily levels are where institutional players make decisions. Intraday levels exist within the context of daily levels.

Look for: price highs and lows that were tested multiple times, areas where price consolidated for multiple sessions, prior breakout levels.

Step 2 — Find the Touches

A level requires at least 2 touches to be meaningful. A single high or low is a data point. Two or more touches at approximately the same price is a zone.

More touches = stronger zone. But: after 4–5 tests, a zone often breaks. Each test depletes the orders waiting at that level.

Step 3 — Draw Zones, Not Lines

Place the zone to capture the bodies of the candles, not the wicks. Wicks represent temporary excursions. Bodies represent where price settled — where buyers and sellers agreed.

Zone width: typically the range of the bodies across all touches at that level. Usually 0.5–1.5% wide on liquid equities.

Test · Retest · Invalidation · Role Reversal
Test

Price approaches a known level. A successful test: price touches the zone and reverses — the zone holds. Buying/selling pressure at the level was sufficient.

Watch for: a reversal candle (hammer, engulfing) forming at the zone with above-average volume.

Retest

After a breakout above resistance, price pulls back to the former resistance zone and holds — the broken resistance now acts as support (role reversal). This retest is often the best entry point for breakout traders.

Why it works: traders who missed the breakout buy the retest. Former sellers at resistance are now buyers at support.

Invalidation

A support zone is invalidated when price closes — not just wicks — decisively below it on above-average volume. A candle close through the zone on high volume means the orders at that level have been exhausted.

One wick through a zone is not invalidation. A closing candle body through the zone on volume is.

Role Reversal (Polarity Flip)
Resistance → Support

When price breaks above a resistance zone and then pulls back, the former resistance zone becomes a support zone. The logic: traders who sold at resistance and were wrong are now buyers at the same level, hoping to cut losses. Traders who missed the breakout buy the dip to the former resistance.

Entry signal: Wait for price to pull back to the former resistance zone and form a bullish reversal candle with volume confirmation.

Support → Resistance

When price breaks below a support zone, the former support becomes resistance. Traders who bought at support and held through the breakdown will sell to break even when price returns to their entry level.

Entry signal: Wait for price to bounce up to the former support zone and form a bearish reversal candle with volume confirmation.

The most common S&R mistake: Treating a level as still valid after it has been cleanly broken. Once a support zone closes below on high volume — it is no longer support. Relabeling it as support because "it was strong before" is anchoring bias. The market has spoken; update your levels.

FINN Application Rules

S&R is context, not signal. A support zone tells you where a reaction may occur. Your candlestick pattern and volume tell you whether to act.

Confluence is the multiplier. A Fibonacci 61.8% level + a prior daily high + a 50-day moving average in the same area = a high-confluence zone. One S&R line in isolation = one data point.

Only close confirmation counts for invalidation. Wicks through levels are not breaks. Candle closes through levels on volume are breaks.

More tests = weaker zone ahead. Each test depletes limit orders sitting at the level. A zone tested 5+ times is close to breaking.
